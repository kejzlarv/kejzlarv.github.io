### Uncertainty Quantification:
Existence of several competing models to describe the same or similar physical process is a common scenario in many scientific fields. In typical applications, researchers decide on which of these models is the best and proceed with their analysis. This approach, however, ignores one important source of uncertainty associated with existence of multiple models, the so-called model uncertainty. I have been recently working on Bayesian Model Averaging (BMA), which is the natural Bayesian framework when facing a number of alternative models. I am particularly interested in challenges that arise in the context of nuclear physics framework. See the preprint:
- Vojtech Kejzlar, Leo Neufcourt,  Taps Maiti, Frederi Viens. _Bayesian averaging of computer models with domain discrepancies: a nuclear physics perspective_. [arXiv:1904.04793](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.04793)

### Variational Inference:

This is currently my main research area. I am interested in extending the state-of-the-art _Variational Bayesian_ methods (an alternative to the standard Bayesian approximation of posterior distributions) in the context of calibration of computer models with Gaussian process emulators. Bayesian calibration with Gaussian process emulators, which is the most popular calibration technique for computer models, is computationally too demanding in situations with complex computer models that rely on many inputs and need to be calibrated with large datasets. On the other hand, variational methods are well scalable and allow for a calibration with large datasets.

### Nuclear Physics:

The field of nuclear physics has been experiencing a robust rump-up of the use of Bayesian statistics in the recent years, especially for the purpose of uncertainty quantification. I am currently involved in several projects with researchers at the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams at MSU and Argonne Notational Laboratory. In particular, I work on robust calibration methods of energy density functionals for nuclei.
